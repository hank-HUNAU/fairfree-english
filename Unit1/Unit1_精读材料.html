1. Introduction
There are some general geological terms that should be understood for exploration and mining purposes. The simplest form of rock strata is that in which each stratum (layer) of rock is superimposed/ˌsjuːpərɪmˈpəʊzd/ 叠置的 on another. These are conformable beds整合层. Movements of the earth's crust and subsequent weathering and rock displacement provide conditions for mineral formation and relocation into the site where it will be found.
The original layers of rock were formed by sedimentary/ˌsedɪˈmentri/ 沉淀物的 or chemical processes with material from higher land masses being redeposited再沉积 in lower areas. The beds could then be distorted into fold/fəʊld/ 褶皱s to produce syncline/ˈsɪŋklaɪn/ 向斜s or anticline背斜s. The scale of those movements is such that a single fold/fəʊld/ 褶皱 producing a synclinal basin may stretch for hundreds of kilometers or may be measured with a wavelength of a few meters.
Where the folding is severe the rock is frequently heavily fractured, particularly where it is under tension on the crest/krest/ 顶部 of an anticlinal stratum or at the base of a synclinal stratum. These weakened zones provide a path for igneous/ˈɪɡniəs/ 火成的 intrusive rocks or mineral rich solutions to permeate/ˈpɜːmieɪt/ 渗透 the host rock and produce ore bodies. It is for this reason that many ore bodies have a typical form, such as the saddle reef鞍礁s. In practice a fault/fɔːlt/ 断层 of this magnitude would have many associated smaller fault/fɔːlt/ 断层s and fractures that can again give rise to an area favourable for subsequent mineralisation.
An unconformity/ˌʌnkənˈfɔːmɪti/ 地层不整合 is also shown in this case produced by an old shore line. These shore lines were often the location of the deposition/ˌdepəˈzɪʃən/ 沉积 of coal seam/siːm/ 层s in the younger geological eras. It can be seen that it is important to distinguish where a coal seam/siːm/ 层 has terminated at the edge of a basin or whether it has been interrupted by a fault/fɔːlt/ 断层 or lost by erosion at an outcrop/ˈaʊtkrɒp/ 露头. Outcrop/ˈaʊtkrɒp/ 露头s occur when a series of beds are cut away by general erosion of the surface or dissect/dɪˈsekt/ 切割ed by rivers, so that the edges of beds are exposed.
After strata have been deposited and mildly disturbed they may be broken up orogenic/ˌɒrəʊˈdʒenɪk/ 造山运动的 movements. These are the large movements of the earth's crust that are associated with mountain building and sometimes cause eruption or intrusion of igneous/ˈɪɡniəs/ 火成的 rock with subsequent mineralisation.
Syngenetic/ˌsɪndʒɪˈnetɪk/ 同生的 deposits are those mineral deposits formed contemporaneously with the parent rock and enclosed by it. They can be igneous/ˈɪɡniəs/ 火成的 or sedimentary/ˌsedɪˈmentri/ 沉淀物的. Epigenetic/ˌepɪdʒɪˈnetɪk/ 后生的 deposits are formed subsequently to the enclosing rock, often by a solution (the mother liquor) penetrating into rocks and depositing minerals. If the solution is a result of magmatic/mæɡˈmætɪk/ 岩浆的 (igneous/ˈɪɡniəs/ 火成的) action it will probably be hot and under pressure and will deposit non-ferrous/ˌnɒnˈferəs/ 有色金属 minerals. If the solution is a result of sedimentary/ˌsedɪˈmentri/ 沉淀物的 action it will percolate/ˈpɜːkəleɪt/ 渗透 downwards causing either chemical changes or direct deposition/ˌdepəˈzɪʃən/ 沉积. Lateritic/ˌlætəˈrɪtɪk/ 红土的 deposits are formed by the leaching away of soluble minerals leaving behind in the laterite a valuable ore, such as nickel or bauxite (aluminium ore). Thus these are normally surface deposits. Although some, such as the iron ores, have been changed and buried so that they are no longer lateritic.
2. Geological Terminology
(1) Ore and Gangue
An orebody may contain both desirable metallic minerals and unwanted non-metallic minerals. The metallic minerals form the ore and the unwanted minerals are known as gangue/ɡæŋ/ 脉石.
(2) Mullock
An orebody should be known more accurately as mineralisation which comprises economic ore and sub-economic lower grade material. The lower grade material broken during mine development is called mullock/ˈmʌlək/ 废石 or waste. These terms are sometimes extended to include host rock broken in development drives.
(3) Disseminated and Massive ores
Where the ore occurs in relatively pure form for example as almost 100 percent lead sulphide/ˈsʌlfaɪd/ 硫化物 (galena/ɡəˈliːnə/ 方铅矿) in even small amounts it is said to be massive/ˈmæsɪv/ 块状的. If however the ore is spread thinly through a body of rock so that individual speck/spek/ 斑点s of the ore are surrounded by a high percentage of rock then this is a disseminated/dɪˈsemɪneɪtɪd/ 浸染状的 ore. Thus if an ore body is sampled over a narrow width of massive/ˈmæsɪv/ 块状的 sulphide/ˈsʌlfaɪd/ 硫化物s it could show a value of lead in the ore sample of 50 to 60 percent (lead in pure galena/ɡəˈliːnə/ 方铅矿 is in fact 86.6 percent). Over a greater width it might only show about 20 percent lead because the sulphide/ˈsʌlfaɪd/ 硫化物s had become disseminated/dɪˈsemɪneɪtɪd/ 浸染状的 or dispersed. The disseminated/dɪˈsemɪneɪtɪd/ 浸染状的 sulphide/ˈsʌlfaɪd/ 硫化物s, assayed separately (i.e. analysed for metal content), may only show as little as 0.2 to 0.5 percent lead.
(4) Cut-off-grade
A gradual transition/trænˈzɪʃən/ 过渡 of high grade to low grade ore is common and it is not always easy to determine the physical limits of an ore body. Consequently it is necessary to determine the lowest value of metallic or non-metallic mineral content which can be mined economically and use this as the physical limit of the orebody. However this limit will obviously vary with the market value of the ore and the mining methods.
(5) Gossan
Where orebodies outcrop/ˈaʊtkrɒp/ 露头 to the surface, particularly in geologically mature areas free of glacial deposition/ˌdepəˈzɪʃən/ 沉积, the portion near to the surface may be leached by impure percolate/ˈpɜːkəleɪt/ 渗透ing waters and subsequently oxidized. Many metallic ores contain a proportion of iron sulphide/ˈsʌlfaɪd/ 硫化物s and these do not leach away but change to iron oxides (haematite/ˈhiːmətaɪt/ 铁矿, goethite/ˈɡɜːθaɪt/ 针铁矿 and jarosite/ˈdʒærəsaɪt/ 黄钾铁矾). The colour of the oxides varies slightly with the original mineral content, but general end result is a yellowish, brownish, or reddish ferruginous/feˈruːdʒɪnəs/ 含铁的 capping known as a gossan/ˈɡɒsn/ 铁帽. This is generally situated directly over the ore body in arid area, such as in Australia, but in some cases it is transported from the outcrop/ˈaʊtkrɒp/ 露头 and has to be traced back.
(6) Supergene Enrichment
The minerals leached away from the outcrop/ˈaʊtkrɒp/ 露头 often cause a zone of secondary enrichment (generally in the form of sulphide/ˈsʌlfaɪd/ 硫化物s) at a depth of 60 to 120m from the surface. This enrichment zone then lies on top of the unaltered primary orebody. Consequently if one in mining or prospecting from the surface for non-ferrous/ˌnɒnˈferəs/ 有色金属 metallic orebodies, there is consequently a transition/trænˈzɪʃən/ 过渡 from oxidized ores, which may need special extractive metallurgical processes, to the supergene/ˈsjuːpədʒiːn/ 表生的 enrichment zone showing high metal contents, and then to the orebody proper which may have a lower metal content again. Thus prospecting with shallow drill holes can give very misleading results.
(7) Placer and Alluvial Deposits
When the primary deposits are brought to the surface by geological methods and then mechanically eroded, they can suffer one of two fates. They may go into solution for subsequent chemical redeposition or they may break up into fairly stable particles which are transported varying distances. Heavy mineral particles, such as gold, settle close to their source. The original falling area, known sometimes as a rock scree/skriː/ 碎石堆 when situated against a mountain slope, is the eluvial/ɪˈljuːviəl/ 残积的 region. Further transportation produces the placer/ˈpleɪsə/ 砂矿 deposits of such as gold and tin, although there appears to be no precise separation of this term from that of alluvial/əˈluːviəl/ 冲积的 deposits. The alluvial/əˈluːviəl/ 冲积的 deposits may be found in existing river beds or in old river beds covered with new deposits such as basalt/ˈbæsɔːlt/ 玄武岩 flows. These buried deposits may be called deep leads as in the state of Victoria, Australia, where much of the gold of the 1850's rush was found.
Transportation to the sea followed by wave action produces beach deposits. Mineral sands is the term applied to the heavy sand deposits of rutile/ˈruːtaɪl/ 金红石, ilmenite/ˈɪlmənaɪt/ 钛铁矿 and zircon/ˈzɜːkɒn/ 锆石, although they are sometimes colloquially called beach sands. These can be on either existing or old shore lines. Other minerals such as tin or diamonds also are found in beach deposits.
(8) Bedded Deposits
Chemical and physical deposition/ˌdepəˈzɪʃən/ 沉积 of consolidated minerals can produce bedded deposit层状矿床s which may extend over larger areas. These are minerals such as coal, iron ore, salt or potash/ˈpɒtæʃ/ 钾碱 deposits, and phosphate/ˈfɒsfeɪt/ 磷酸盐s. Limestone required for cement or blast furnace is also a bedded deposit层状矿床. Although the dip (slopes) of these bedded deposit层状矿床s can be steep, and the beds are broken by fault/fɔːlt/ 断层s or other geological disturbances, their lateral extent is usually larger and more continuous than is that of the metallic non-ferrous/ˌnɒnˈferəs/ 有色金属 deposits.
